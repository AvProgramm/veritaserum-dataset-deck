Part 1 · Why build something new

Gaps that could motivate a new dataset

① Temporal leakage

Most natural-claim sets include evidence published after the claim - models look up verdicts instead of verifying. Only AVeriTeC filters it.

② Document-level scarcity

Almost everything verifies isolated sentences. No public corpus labels whole documents - we had to hand-label ours.

③ Thin SEA coverage

Verification data clusters in high-resource languages; Southeast Asia has only Vietnamese.

④ Label chaos

MultiFC ≈150 rating schemes; X-Fact collapses to 7. Mappings are lossy.

⑤ Numerical claims

QuanTemp shows models fail exactly where misinformation concentrates.

⑥ Static & stale

Links rot; claims age. Dynamic benchmarks only now emerging.

The green three are our sweet spot - they can be attacked together. (Also: synthetic-claim bias, unannotated evidence quality.)

Spend the most time here - this is the "why a new dataset" argument. The three highlighted gaps are the ones a single well-designed dataset can hit at once.
Part 1 · Conclusion

The opening

3 gaps, 1 dataset

A document-level, temporally filtered, evidence-grounded verification dataset in an underrepresented language addresses temporal leakage, document-level scarcity, and SEA coverage simultaneously - at a scale a student team can annotate.

This is the thesis of the whole review. Everything in Parts 2 and 3 is about making this concrete: which domain, which language.

Part 2 · The two winners

Best effort-to-quality ratio

ClaimReview harvesting

Google's ClaimReview structured markup exposes machine-readable claim + verdict + publisher across PolitiFact, Snopes, AAP FactCheck, Full Fact.

  • Labels come free from professionals
  • Collection is a parsing job, not annotation
  • watchmust exclude the fact-check itself (leakage)

Statistics-backed claims

Quantitative claims (economy, sport, demographics) check against official structured data - ABS, World Bank, league tables.

  • Verification is often a lookup
  • Labels near-objective; targets the numerical gap
  • watchclaim phrasing vs. metric definition
These two combine well: ClaimReview for volume of labelled claims, statistics for evidence that provably predates the claim.
Part 3 · Underrepresented languages

Scoring Southeast Asia on three criteria

A · Datasets

Does an evidence-grounded verification benchmark exist (not just classification or retrieval)?

B · Models

Are suitable AI models available - regional LLMs, encoders, toolkits?

C · Sources

Are there active claim + evidence sources - fact-check orgs, media, data portals?

Candidates assessed: Malay, Indonesian, Vietnamese, Thai, Filipino, and the low-resource trio (Burmese, Khmer, Lao).

These three criteria come straight from the task brief. Every language on the next slides gets scored A/B/C.
Part 3 · The lead candidate

Malay - Bahasa Melayu (~30M+ speakers)

A · Datasets - wide open

No evidence-grounded verification set exists. MalCov is COVID classification; Malay in MultiClaim (retrieval) & AMC-16K (pairing) only. X-Fact skips Malay entirely.

B · Models - good

SEA-LION v4 (Gemma-3-27B / Qwen-3-32B, open), MaLLaM (Malay-specific, self-hostable), the Malaya toolkit, plus XLM-R / mBERT.

C · Sources - strong

Sebenarnya.my (national portal since 2017), JomCheck, AFP Malaysia for claims; DOSM stats, Malay Wikipedia, media for evidence.

Bonus: high mutual intelligibility with Indonesian roughly doubles usable evidence and enables model transfer.

The core of the recommendation. Malay is unique: a total dataset vacuum but everything else - models and sources - is in place. That combination is what makes it buildable.
Part 3 · The alternatives

Other Southeast Asian candidates

Indonesian (~200M)

Best-resourced (IndoBERT, NusaCrowd, MAFINDO/TurnBackHoax). But classification sets already exist and local NLP competition is strong - less novel.

Vietnamese (~85M)

ViFactCheck (AAAI 2025) already fills the gap. Mature PhoBERT models. Low novelty remaining.

Thai (~60M) · Filipino (~80M)

Both viable runners-up. Thai: script segmentation friction, no team reader. Filipino: Tsek.ph / VeraFiles active, heavy English code-switching.

Burmese · Khmer · Lao

Wide-open gap but under-resourced on models and sources - impractical on a student timeline.

Vietnamese is the cautionary tale - it WAS the obvious target until Dec 2024. Indonesian is the strongest alternative but more crowded. The low-resource trio is high-impact, low-feasibility.

Recommendation

Build for Malay

The only major SEA language with zero evidence-grounded verification data, yet with government-backed and independent fact-checkers to harvest, open regional LLMs for baselines, and Indonesian adjacency for transfer. ViFactCheck proves the recipe (~7k pairs, κ≈0.83, top-venue) is student-scale.

State it plainly. If asked "why not Indonesian" - more prior art, more competition. "Why not Vietnamese" - already done. Malay is the open goal.
Proposed direction · for discussion

A bilingual Malay–English document-level dataset

  • Claims from Sebenarnya.my / JomCheck / AFP Malaysia; fact-check articles used for labels only, never as evidence.
  • Evidence = open-web docs published before each claim (AVeriTeC protocol), retrieved via our Tavily pipeline, archived against link rot.
  • Two granularities: per-claim (S/R/NEI) and per-document (mostly_supported / mostly_refuted / mixed / unverified) - the exact scheme our backend already uses.
  • Baselines: SEA-LION v4 and Qwen3 8B through our existing /factcheck/document endpoint - a paper-shaped evaluation with no new infrastructure.

Hits three documented gaps at once, and every component maps onto what this repo already has.

This is the actionable ask. Note how each bullet reuses existing Veritaserum infrastructure - that's what makes it realistic for our timeline.
